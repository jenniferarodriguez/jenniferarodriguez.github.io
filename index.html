Research

Detection of Diffuse Hot Gas around the Young, Potential Superstar Cluster H72.97-69.39

First-author: Trinity L. Webb
Co-authors: Jennifer A. Rodriguez, Laura A. Lopez, Anna L. Rosen, Lachlan Lancaster, Omnarayani Nayak, Anna F. McLeod, Paarmita Pandey, and Grace M. Olivier.
In this project, we analyzed the first Chandra X-ray observations of H72.97-69.39, a young proto-superstar cluster in the N79 star-forming region of the Large Magellanic Cloud. We detect hard X-ray emission that aligns with young stellar objects identified with JWST and fills cavities in the dense gas mapped by ALMA. Our results suggest that stellar wind feedback generates hot gas early in star cluster formation. However, the observed X-ray luminosity is significantly lower than expected, indicating energy is lost rapidly through turbulent mixing, radiative cooling, or physical leakage.
Paper can be found here: https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2024ApJ...977...45W/abstract

GS 2000+25: The Least Luminous Black Hole X-ray Binary

First-author: Jennifer A. Rodriguez
Co-authors: Ryan Urquhart, Richard M. Plotkin, Teresa Panurach, Laura Chomiuk, Jay Strader, James C. A. Miller-Jones, Elena Gallo, and Gregory R. Sivakoff.
In this project, we analyzed the first simultaneous radio and X-ray observations of GS 2000+25, a quiescent black hole X-ray binary. Our results show this system is faint in X-rays, making it the least luminous known quiescent black hole X-ray binary. No radio emission was detected, providing constraints on the relationship between accretion and jets at low luminosities. GS 2000+25, along with four other quiescent stellar-mass black holes, exhibit radio to X-ray luminosity ratios that align with, though slightly below, the low-state radio/X-ray correlation observed in more luminous stellar-mass black holes.
Paper can be found here: https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2020ApJ...889...58R/abstract

Outreach

I am passionate about fostering a culture that embraces and promotes diversity, equity, and inclusion. I am involved in and lead various organizations that support underrepresented minorities in STEM, particularly in Astronomy and Physics.
Read about the statistics on underrepresented groups in physics from the American Institue of Physics here.

AstroLatinX

I am one of the co-founders of AstroLatinX. Our goal is to introduce Hispanic/Latinx students and families to astronomy and STEM by providing educational opportunities to underrepresented and low-income groups in Columbus and surrounding communities. AstroLatinX is co-led by OSU graduate students Elvira Cruz-Cruz, Xavier Lesley, Sebastian Lopez, Jennifer Rodriguez, and OSU undergrad Gregory Costa, and is co-advised by Drs. Laura Lopez and Wayne Schlingman.
Together, we developed and presented the first Spanish planetarium shows at OSU, enhancing STEM accessibility for non-English speakers. Additionally, we created and presented the first planetarium shows on indigenous cultures and their constellations at OSU, emphasizing the significance of indigenous astronomy and the preservation on lost knowledge.


Astro Café

I am a co-founder of Astro Café, a Spanish journal club designed to offer a judgment- and stress-free environment for practicing astronomy research discussions in Spanish.
Astro Café is open to individuals from any institution, at any career stage, and with any level of Spanish proficiency.


Polaris

I was a mentor for the Polaris Mentorship Course at OSU, which pairs 1st and 2nd year underrepresented Physics and Astronomy undergraduate students with graduate student mentors. This year-long course covers topics like imposter syndrome and science communication in the fall, and a mini research project in the spring.
